Heart Locker

An exercise in "design noir" principles. How can we design for death? How can a household object remind people of death?

Design I

The brief for this project asked us to "design for death" as an exercise in Design Noir.

The Heart Locker is an externalized representation of a person’s pulse. The user wears a small piece of jewelry (e.g. a watch) equipped with a sensor that monitors their heartbeat and transmits the collected information, in real time, to the locker component. The locker is a glass jar that contains a suspended, beating heart. It uses data collected by the watch sensor to inform the rhythm of the heart.

Design II

The mapping between the user’s pulse and the rhythm of the heart in the locker is neither constant nor predictable. The device randomly interferes with the received data in order to introduce deceptive “false” moments of slowness or excitement. The incidence of these moments increases according to the distance between the user and the locker: when he is far away, there is a greater likelihood of interference.

Because the locker provides only a partial glimpse into the subject’s state, the people around it must use their imaginations to complete the story. As the pulse changes from moment to moment, it plays on the natural human drive to make meaning and provokes scrutiny the subject’s physical and mental state.

Implementation

The Heart Locker object shown in the concept video is made from balloons, tape, red paint, a hand pump, and a cookie jar (thanks Mom). The video mocks up the sensing and processing of biometric data in order to portray the complete interaction.
